CASE STUDY · BUSINESS AUTOMATION · B2B SAAS

Cutting weekly reporting from three hours to forty-five minutes.

A 15-person B2B SaaS company was burning three hours of senior time every Monday assembling a client report by hand. We built an automated pipeline that does the assembly and drafts the narrative — and gave them their Mondays back.

Client
B2B SaaS · 15 people
Service
Automation + AI
Timeline
6 weeks
Model
Fixed-price

Every Monday morning, the head of operations at a B2B analytics SaaS company sat down to build the same report. Pull numbers from the CRM. Cross-reference the product database. Open three spreadsheets. Reconcile the figures. Write the narrative summary. Format it. Send it. Three hours, every week, on work that produced nothing new — it just moved existing data from one place to another.

The first thing we did was not build anything. We mapped the actual process and found that two of the three spreadsheets contained data that already lived in the product database — they existed only because, years earlier, someone didn't have database access and started keeping their own copy. The process had calcified around a workaround nobody remembered the reason for.

What we built

An automated pipeline that pulls directly from the two real data sources — the CRM and the product database — on a schedule. It assembles the figures, runs them through a templated set of calculations, and uses an LLM to draft the narrative summary in the same voice and structure the team had been writing by hand. The output lands in their inbox Monday morning as a near-final draft.

Python n8n OpenAI PostgreSQL REST APIs Cron

What we deliberately didn't build

We didn't automate the send. The team wanted a human to review the report before it reached the client — and they were right to. Automating the last mile would have saved five minutes and added real risk. The review step stays human by design.

What changed

Report assembly dropped from roughly three hours to about forty-five minutes — and most of that forty-five minutes is the human review we kept on purpose. The head of operations got roughly ten hours a month back. The two redundant spreadsheets were retired. And the report quality actually improved, because the automated version doesn't make the transcription errors that creep into manual data assembly at 9am on a Monday.

The system has run every week since handover. The team owns it, can adjust the template themselves, and hasn't needed us since the 30-day support window closed.

IMPLEMENTATION NOTE · BY AREES SHAH

When to build custom AI and when to just use the API.

The most expensive mistake we see businesses make with AI isn't picking the wrong model. It's building a custom solution when a well-placed API call would have done the job — or the reverse.

A prospect came to us last quarter wanting a custom-trained model. They'd read that fine-tuning was how serious companies did AI, and they wanted the serious version. The problem they described — classifying incoming support tickets by topic and urgency — needed nothing of the sort. A well-structured prompt to an existing API, wired into their ticketing system, solved it in a fraction of the time and a fraction of the cost.

Here's the decision we actually run through. Most business problems that look like they need custom AI need one of three things instead: a good API call placed in the right workflow, a retrieval system over the company's own data, or simply a better-defined process that doesn't need AI at all. Custom model work — real fine-tuning or training — is the right answer in a narrow band of cases: when you have proprietary data that genuinely changes model behavior, when latency or cost at scale rules out API calls, or when you need the model to do something the foundation models simply can't.

The reason this matters isn't purist. It's economic. A custom model carries a permanent maintenance cost — retraining, evaluation, infrastructure — that a business should only take on when the value clearly justifies it. For most companies, most of the time, the API is not the compromise. It's the correct engineering decision.

So when a client asks us to build custom AI, the first thing we do is try to talk them out of it. If they still need it after that conversation, then we know it's real — and we build it properly.

INDUSTRY PERSPECTIVE · BY AREES SHAH

The automation trap: why most workflow projects fail before they start.

Automation promises to save time. But automating a process you've never actually examined usually just helps you do the wrong thing faster — and at greater scale.

There's a specific failure mode we've learned to watch for. A client comes to us with a process they want automated. They've done it manually for years; they know it cold. They just want the manual steps replaced with a system. It sounds like the most straightforward kind of project there is.

It's a trap, and here's why. A process that's been done manually for years has usually accumulated workarounds, redundant steps, and decisions nobody remembers making. When a human runs it, they silently correct for these — they skip the step that doesn't matter anymore, they catch the obvious error, they apply judgment the documented process never captured. Automate it literally, and you encode every one of those flaws into a system that now runs them faithfully, at speed, with no human catching the problems.

So before we automate anything, we map what's actually happening — not what the documented process says, but what people actually do. Almost every time, we find steps that exist only because of a constraint that no longer applies, data being copied between places it doesn't need to be, and at least one decision point where the "rule" is really just one person's judgment that needs to be made explicit before a machine can apply it.

The automation is the easy part. The valuable part is the examination that comes first. Fix the process, then automate the fixed version. Skip that step, and you've spent good money making a bad process permanent.

REFERENCE GUIDE · BY AREES SHAH

What actually belongs in a SaaS MVP — and what kills your timeline.

The fastest way to blow your runway before launch is to build a v1 that's actually a v3. Here's the scoping discipline that gets you to a real, testable product without the bloat.

Every founder we work with has a feature list. And almost every feature list is too long — not because the features are bad, but because most of them are answers to questions the market hasn't asked yet. An MVP exists to answer one question: will people use and pay for the core thing? Everything that doesn't serve that question is weight you're carrying before you've earned the right to carry it.

The features founders insist on but rarely need at MVP: elaborate settings and customization, multiple user roles before there are multiple users, an admin dashboard with analytics nobody will read yet, integrations with tools the first users may not even use, and a polished onboarding flow for a product whose value isn't proven. Each of these feels essential. None of them validate the core idea, and together they can double your timeline.

The infrastructure founders skip but shouldn't: clean data architecture (because migrating a messy schema later is brutal), basic auth done properly (because retrofitting security is worse than building it), and a deployment setup that lets you ship updates without drama. These don't feel like features, so they get cut. They're the things that determine whether your v2 is a pleasure or a nightmare.

The discipline is simple to state and hard to hold: build the smallest thing that genuinely tests the core hypothesis, on a foundation solid enough that success doesn't force a rebuild. When we scope an MVP, half the work is helping the founder decide what to leave out. It's the most valuable thing we do before writing any code.
